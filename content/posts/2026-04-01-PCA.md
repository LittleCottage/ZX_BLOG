## Background
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimension reduction technique that receives applications in many scientific disciplines.  
Nevertheless, I have seen so many non-rigorous, if not completely wrong, usages on its associated terminologies, math derivations, 
interpretations, etc.  The aim of this article is to clarify some most fundamental concepts and definitions related to PCA. 

## Principal Component Directions vs. Principal Components
This is arguably the most serious misconception in PCA, even experienced statisticians and machine learning engineers confused them: 
practitioners tend to referring "principal component directions" as "principal components" or vice versa 
(e.g. [<a href='#geron2022handson'>1</a>], p. 224). Such confusion is very analogous to linear algebra students who cannot distinguish
"basis" and "coordinates (under that basis)" clearly.  A deep understanding on this linear algebra topic would help clarify on the 
differences and connections between "PC directions" and "PCs".  

To understand the core idea underlying the PCA, it is also important to clarify the meaning of the term "variance" under the PCA context. 
The "variance" appeared in the sentence such as "we are looking for the direction $v_1$ such that the variance of $Xv_1$ is maximized" 
should be interpreted as the **sample variance** of the dataset $D_1 := \\{\langle x_1, v_1\rangle, \ldots, \langle x_n, v_1\rangle\\}$. 
In other words, the PCA does not require assuming any underlying distribution of the data matrix 
$X = \begin{bmatrix} x_1^\top \\\\ \vdots \\\\ x_n^\top \end{bmatrix}$, as most statistical models do. This means that the PCA can be fully explained and demonstrated by 
linear algebra tools only without invoking any probability or statistics knowledge. In fact, as will be seen later, the term "variance" is 
completely superfluous and may be replaced with the more accurate and expressive term "norm". 

That said, let's lay out classical definitions and procedures related to the PCA: 

> Let $X = \begin{bmatrix} x_{ij}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1^\top \\\\ \vdots \\\\ x_n^\top \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ be a centered design matrix such that 
> $\sum\limits_{i = 1}^n x_{ij} = 0$ for every $j \in \\{1, \ldots, p\\}$ (namely, $e^\top X = 0^\top$ where $e := (1, \ldots, 1) \in \mathbb{R}^n$). By definition, the 
> **first principal component direction** $v_1$ is a unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^p$ such that the Euclidean norm of the vector
>  $(x_1^\top v, \ldots, x_n^\top v)^\top =: Xv$ is maximized over the unit sphere $\\{v: \\|v\\| = 1\\}$. In other words,
>
> $$ v_1 = \argmax_{\\|v\\| = 1} \\|Xv\\|^2 = \argmax_{\\|v\\| = 1} v^\top X^\top X v.$$ 
> The vector $z_1 = Xv_1 = (x_1^\top v_1, \ldots, x_n^\top v_1)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^n$, which may be regarded as the collection of magnitudes of projections 
> $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ onto the direction $v_1$, is called the **first principal component** of $X$.
>
> Having found $v_1, \ldots, v_{j - 1} \in \mathbb{R}^p$, the **$j$-th principal component direction** $v_j$ is defined as (where $L(v_1, \ldots, v_{j - 1})$ is the subspace 
> spanned by $\\{v_1, \ldots, v_{j - 1}\\}$):
> $$ v_j = \argmax_{\substack{\\|v\\| = 1 \\\\ v \perp L(v_1, \ldots, v_{j - 1})}} \\|Xv\\|^2, \quad j = 2, \ldots, p.$$
> Accordingly, $z_j = Xv_j$ is called the **$j$-th principal component** of $X$. 

From the pure linear algebra perspective, the "centered" condition of $X$ is not essential -- without imposing it, $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ can be defined in exactly the same manner.
The reason of requiring it is purely statistical: it is this condition that permits us to interpret the quadratic form $v^\top X^\top X v$ as "variance of the projected data" 
because the sample mean of $\\{x_1^\top v, \ldots, x_n^\top v\\}$ is given by $\bar{z} := \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i^\top v/n = e^\top Xv/n = 0$ whence
$$
\begin{align*}
v^\top X^\top Xv = \sum_{i = 1}^n(x_i^\top v - \bar{z})^2 = (n - 1)\hat{\sigma}^2(x_1^\top v, \ldots, x_n^\top v).
\end{align*}
$$

That $v_1, \ldots, v_p$ defined above are $p$ orthonormal eigenvectors of the matrix $X^\top X$ is a classical linear algebra result so I won't repeat the proof here (for example, 
see [<a href='#li1988linear'>2</a>], Section 7.6, Theorem 4). It is also well-known that $v_j^\top X^\top Xv_j$ equals to the $j$-th eigenvalue of $X^\top X$, where $\lambda_1 \geq
\lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_p > 0$. 

As mentioned earlier and should be crystal clear now, the **principal component directions** $\\{v_1, \ldots, v_p\\}$ form an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^p$,
whereas the **principal components** are collections of magnitudes of projected observations onto all the basis vectors. Although this is not strictly the same as the relationship
between "basis" and "coordinates" in linear algebra, the principal components do play the role of coordinates when we try to express an original datum under the new orthonormal 
basis (i.e., the principal component directions). For instance, we can talk about the coordinate of $x_i$ under $V := \\{v_1, \ldots, v_p\\}$, which can 
be expressed in terms of principal components as follows:
$$
\begin{align*}
x_i = \langle x_i, v_1\rangle v_1 + \cdots + \langle x_i, v_p\rangle v_p = z_1[i]v_1 + \cdots + z_p[i]v_p, \tag{1}
\end{align*}
$$
where $z[i]$ denotes the $i$-th component of $z$. 

By comparison, under the natural basis $\\{\varepsilon_1, \ldots, \varepsilon_p\\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^p$, one has:
$$
\begin{align*}
x_i = \langle x_i, \varepsilon_1\rangle \varepsilon_1 + \cdots + \langle x_i, \varepsilon_p\rangle \varepsilon_p = x_i[1]\varepsilon_1 + \cdots + x_i[p]\varepsilon_p. \tag{2}
\end{align*}
$$
Comparing (1) and (2) would make a better sense of interpreting principal components as "coordinates": in (2), $(x_i[1], \ldots, x_i[p])$ is exactly the coordinate of the vector 
$x_i$ under $\\{\varepsilon_1, \ldots, \varepsilon_p\\}$. Albeit the coordinate $(z_1[i], \ldots, z_p[i])$ of $x_i$ under $V$ is not any single principal component $z_j$ (which by 
definition is $n$-dimensional), it can be and should be interpreted as $(\pi_i(z_1), \ldots, \pi_i(z_p))$, where $\pi_i: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is the $i$-th 
*coordinate function* (see [<a href='#rudin1976principles'>3</a>], p. 88). That is, the coordinate of $x_i$ under $V$ is a function of combined principal components
 $\\{z_1, \ldots, z_p\\}$. In this sense, the principal components may still be loosely understood as coordinates with respect to the new basis $V$.  

$(1)$ and $(2)$ can be better reconciled if we take a traditional linear model perspective. For clarity, rewrite the observed design matrix as $\mathcal{X} = \begin{bmatrix} 
\boldsymbol{x}^{(1)\top} \\\\ \vdots \\\\ \boldsymbol{x}^{(n)\top}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{x}_1 & \cdots & \boldsymbol{x}_p\end{bmatrix}$,
and rewrite $z_j$ as $\boldsymbol{z}_j$, $j = 1, \ldots, p$ (also boldface all the other vectors). $(1)$ and $(2)$ can then be rewritten as
\begin{align*}
\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} = \langle \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}, \boldsymbol{v}_1\rangle \boldsymbol{v}_1 + \cdots + \langle \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}, \boldsymbol{v}_p\rangle \boldsymbol{v}_p 
= \boldsymbol{z}_1[i]\boldsymbol{v}_1 + \cdots + \boldsymbol{z}_p[i]\boldsymbol{v}_p. \tag{1'}
\end{align*}
\begin{align*}
\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} = \langle \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_1\rangle \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_1 + \cdots + \langle \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}, 
\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_p\rangle\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_p 
= \boldsymbol{x}_1[i]\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_1 + \cdots + \boldsymbol{x}_p[i]\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_p. \tag{2'}
\end{align*}

For the purpose of dimension-reduction or visualization, terms with index bigger than $k$, where $k \ll p$, are dropped, yielding the approximation
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} \approx \boldsymbol{z}_1[i]\boldsymbol{v}_1 + \cdots + \boldsymbol{z}_k[i]\boldsymbol{v}_k. 
\end{equation*}
In a visualization task, the $p$-dim point $\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}$ is then represented by a $k$-dim point $(\boldsymbol{z}_1[i], \ldots, \boldsymbol{z}_k[i]) \in \mathbb{R}^k$
(typically $k = 2$ or $3$). One common abuse of notation is that in such plots, the axes are usually labeled as "PC1, PC2" (e.g., [<a href='#hardle2024applied'>4</a>], Figure 11.3),
which is better to be replaced by "PCD1, PCD2".  This is the very purpose of this vignette -- to clarify the difference between the "PC" and the "PC Direction".

How much is the approximation error? At the population-level (see Section 4 for details), the analysis goes as follows: denote $X^\top\upsilon_1\upsilon_1 + \cdots + 
X^\top\upsilon_k\upsilon_k$ by $\hat{X}$, then the remainder term $\delta := X - \hat{X} = \sum_{l = k + 1}^pX^\top\upsilon_l\upsilon_l$. By the orthogonality, it then
follows that $\delta^2 = \sum_{l = k + 1}^p\upsilon^\top XX^\top\upsilon$, whence the expected squared error is $E[\delta^2] = \sum_{l = k + 1}^p\upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon
= \sum_{l = k + 1}^p\lambda_l$. This gives a better explanation to the rule of choosing $k$ such that $\sum_{l = 1}^k \lambda_l/\sum_{l = 1}^p\lambda_l$ should be large (say,
greater than $90\\%$) -- the $k$ should be sufficiently large such that the *relative approximation error* $E[\delta^2]/E[\\|X\||^2]$ is bounded below by $10\\%$. I found this
reasoning is much more intuitive than the traditional "percentage of total variation explained" explanation.

## Determining PC Directions via SVD
In the machine learning literature, PC directions are usually determined by finding the singular value decomposition (SVD) of $X$ directly (as opposed to computing $X^\top X$ 
first, then finding the spectral decomposition of $X^\top X$, which is the way I learned as a undergraduate student). More explicitly, we have 
> Let $X = U\Sigma V^\top$ be the SVD of $X$, where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}$ are orthogonal matrices. Then the $p$ columns of $V$
> are all the $p$ PC directions with respect to $X$.

The proof is straightforward:  Given $X = U\Sigma V^\top$, it follows that $X^\top X = V \Sigma^\top U^\top U\Sigma V^\top = V\Sigma^\top\Sigma V^\top$, namely, the spectral
decomposition of $X^\top X$ is $V\Sigma^\top\Sigma V^\top$.  This means that columns of $V$ are all the eigenvectors of $X^\top X$, thereby they are $p$ principal component 
directions with respect to $X$.

## Further Discussions
 PCA is usually taught directly in terms of the definition above, but it also has a classical routine that gives its population-level formulation first.  For a given 
  $p$-dimensional zero-mean random vector $X = (X_1, \ldots, X_p)^\top$ with covariance matrix $\Sigma$, the *first population-level principle component direction* $\upsilon_1$ is a unit 
  vector in $\mathbb{R}^p$ such that the variance of the *standardized linear combination* of $X$ is maximized, namely, 
  \begin{equation*}
  \upsilon_1 = \argmax_{\\|\upsilon\\| = 1}\operatorname{Var}(\upsilon^\top X) = \argmax_{\\|\upsilon\\| = 1}\upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon.
  \end{equation*}
  It seems not very intuitive why maximizing $\operatorname{Var}(\upsilon^\top X)$ is chosen to be the objective 
  function (at least to me). In comparison, it is much more intuitive to choose the unit direction $\upsilon$ such 
  that the expected distance (aka, norm) in $\mathbb{R}^p$ between $X$ and its projection onto the space $L(\upsilon)$, which equals to $\langle X, \upsilon\rangle\upsilon = \upsilon^\top X \upsilon = \upsilon\upsilon^\top X$, is minimized. That is, $\upsilon_1$ minimizes 
  \begin{equation*}
  E[\\|X - \upsilon\upsilon^\top X\\|^2] = E[\\|(I_{(p)} - \upsilon\upsilon^\top)X\\|^2]
  = E[X^\top(I_{(p)} - \upsilon\upsilon^\top)X] 
  = \operatorname{tr}((I_{(p)} - \upsilon\upsilon^\top)\Sigma) 
  = p - \upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon.
  \end{equation*}
  Therefore to minimize $\\|X - \upsilon\upsilon^\top X\\|^2$ is equivalent to maximize $\upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon$. At the population-level, it seems hard to find the 
  counterpart of the "first principal component $z_1$", probably due to that $z_1$ is observation-dependent (for instance, it is $n$-dimensional, where $n$ is sample size). 
  If we have to, it might not be a bad idea to define $Z_1 := \upsilon_1^\top X$ as the *first population-level principal component*, but it is a scalar. 

  Now suppose we want to approximate $X$ by a projection of $X$ onto a 2-dimensional subspace $V_2$ (in $\mathbb{R}^p$) containing $L(\upsilon_1)$. 
  Clearly, such subspace can be expressed as $L(\upsilon_1, \upsilon)$ with $\upsilon \perp \upsilon_1$ and $\\|\upsilon\\| = 1$, and the projection 
  of $X$ (at this moment simply view $X$ as a vector in $\mathbb{R}^p$ instead of a random vector in the $L^2$ space) onto $L(\upsilon_1, \upsilon)$ is 
  $\hat{X} = \langle\upsilon_1, X\rangle\upsilon_1 + \langle\upsilon, X\rangle\upsilon = (\upsilon_1\upsilon_1^\top + \upsilon\upsilon^\top)X$. As before, 
  a reasonable choice of $\upsilon_2$ should be such that the expected distance between $X$ and $\hat{X}$ is minimized, i.e., $\upsilon_2$ minimizes 
  \begin{equation*}
  E[\\|X - \hat{X}\\|^2] = E[X^\top(I_{(p)} - \upsilon_1\upsilon_1^\top - \upsilon\upsilon^\top)X]
  = p - \upsilon_1^\top\Sigma\upsilon_1 - \upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon.
  \end{equation*}
  Hence $\upsilon_2 = \argmax_{\\|\upsilon\\| = 1, \upsilon \perp \upsilon_1}\upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon$. 
  
  The population-level principal component directions $\upsilon_3, \ldots, \upsilon_p$ can be obtained as their standard forms by a similar argument. 

  Now that we have defined $p$ population-level PC directions by maximizing the quadratic form $\upsilon^\top\Sigma\upsilon$ under different constraints, 
  it is convenient to derive sample-level PC directions (those we are familiar with) by replacing "$\Sigma$" in the objective function with its estimate 
  $\mathcal{X}^\top\mathcal{X}/n$, where $\mathcal{X} = [x_{ij}] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ is a centered data matrix (confusingly, the $\mathcal{X}$ here 
  is exactly the "$X$" in preceding sections). This classical recipe ubiquitously adopted in statistical inference 
  then brings us back to the definition of "principal component directions" in the definition in Section 2. 

 What is less transparent is the connection between the "population-level PC $Z_j$" (later we will update its definition) and the "sample-level PC $z_j$". To this end, let
 \begin{equation*}
 Z := \begin{bmatrix} Z_1 \\\\ Z_2 \\\\ \vdots \\\\ Z_p \end{bmatrix} = \Upsilon^\top X  \tag{$\dagger$}
 \end{equation*} 
 be the *PC transformation* of $X$ ([<a href='#hardle2024applied'>4</a>], Theorem 11.1), which is well-defined at the population-level. From another (more easily generalizable)
 perspective, we view $\Upsilon^\top$ as a linear transformation from $\mathbb{R}^p$ to $\mathbb{R}^p$, which sends the coordinate of $X$ under the standard basis 
 $\mathscr{B}_1 = \\{\varepsilon_1, \ldots, \varepsilon_p\\}$ to the coordinate of $X$ under the new orthonormal basis $\mathscr{B}_2 = \\{\upsilon_1, \ldots, \upsilon_p\\}$.
 In linear algebra terminology, since $\Upsilon$ is the *transition matrix* from $\mathscr{B}_1$ to $\mathscr{B}_2$, $(\dagger)$ is exactly the 
 *coordinate transformation equation* ([<a href='#li1988linear'>2</a>], Theorem 4.4.2) from $\mathscr{B}_1$ to $\mathscr{B}_2$ (note $\Upsilon$ is orthogonal so 
 $\Upsilon^{-1} = \Upsilon^\top$). 

But we know *principal components* are vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, where $n$ is the number of observations drawn from $X$. As such, for $n$ i.i.d. random vectors 
$X^{(1)}, \ldots, X^{(n)} \sim X$, let $Z^{(i)} = \begin{bmatrix} Z_1^{(i)} & \cdots & Z_p^{(i)}\end{bmatrix}^\top$ be the coordinate of $X^{(i)}$ with respect to 
$\mathscr{B}_2$, $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Then we have 
 \begin{align*}
 \begin{bmatrix} X^{(1)} & \cdots & X^{(n)}\end{bmatrix} &= 
 \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 & \cdots & \varepsilon_p \end{bmatrix}
 \begin{bmatrix} X_1^{(1)} & \cdots & X_1^{(n)} \\\\ X_2^{(1)} & \cdots & X_2^{(n)} \\\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\\\ X_p^{(1)} & \cdots & X_p^{(n)}\end{bmatrix} 
 =: \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 & \cdots & \varepsilon_p \end{bmatrix}
 \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{X}_1^\top \\\\ \vdots \\\\ \boldsymbol{X}_p^\top \end{bmatrix} \\\\
 &= \begin{bmatrix} \upsilon_1 & \cdots & \upsilon_p\end{bmatrix}
 \begin{bmatrix} Z_1^{(1)} & \cdots & Z_1^{(n)} \\\\ Z_2^{(1)} & \cdots & Z_2^{(n)} \\\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\\\ Z_p^{(1)} & \cdots & Z_p^{(n)}\end{bmatrix}
 =: \begin{bmatrix} \upsilon_1 & \cdots & \upsilon_p \end{bmatrix}
 \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_1^\top \\\\ \vdots \\\\ \boldsymbol{Z}_p^\top \end{bmatrix}.
 \end{align*}
 This implies that 
 \begin{equation}
 \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_1 & \cdots & \boldsymbol{Z}_p \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{X}_1 & \cdots & \boldsymbol{X}_p\end{bmatrix}\Upsilon. \tag{3}
 \end{equation}
 So if we view $\\{\boldsymbol{X}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{X}_p\\}$ as a basis of the $p$-dimensional subspace $S$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (as for the geometry of linear models), this means
 that $\\{\boldsymbol{Z}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{Z}_p\\}$ is also a basis of $S$. Therefore, at the (semi-)population-level, $\\{\boldsymbol{Z}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{Z}_p\\}$ may
 be recognized as a new basis of $S$ which is obtained by rotating the original basis $\\{\boldsymbol{X}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{X}_p\\}$. The sample-level principal components 
 $\\{z_1, \ldots, z_p\\}$ thus can be regarded as its sample-level counterpart after substituting $\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{X}_1 & \cdots & \boldsymbol{X}_p\end{bmatrix}$ and 
 $\Upsilon$ in $(3)$ for the observed design matrix $\mathcal{X}$ and estimate $\hat{\Upsilon} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & \cdots & v_p\end{bmatrix}$ respectively, i.e., $z_j = 
 \hat{\boldsymbol{Z}}_j = \mathcal{X}v_j$, $j = 1, \ldots, p$.

  Lastly, let's just mention an interesting representation of $z_j$ which is $z_j = \sqrt{\lambda_j}u_j$, where $u_j$ is the $j$-th column of $U$ in 
  the SVD of $\mathcal{X} = U\Lambda V^\top = \sum_{l = 1}^p \sqrt{\lambda_l}u_lv_l^\top$ (assume $\operatorname{rank}(\mathcal{X}) = p$). The proof is easy: by definition,
  \begin{equation}
  z_j = \mathcal{X}v_j = \sum_{l = 1}^p\sqrt{\lambda_l}u_lv_l^\top v_j = \sqrt{\lambda_j}u_j. \tag{4}
  \end{equation}

  Comparing (4) with the $j$-th principal component direction $v_j$, it appears that the PC direction and the PC itself possess some sort of *duality*.

## Conclusion
Unlike the traditional perspective of considering PCA as the most important dimension-reduction technique, I am more inclined to view it as a data-driven basis-searching algorithm 
that enjoys the aforementioned properties.  It is precisely the orthogonality and the rank ordering of the new basis that endows the merits of the PCA in applications.
